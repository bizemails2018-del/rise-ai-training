People confuse these two constantly. Agency is the ability to act ‚Äî having hands. Autonomy is the permission to act without asking ‚Äî being allowed to use those hands. An agent can be highly capable (high agency) but tightly controlled (low autonomy). Our approach: start with high agency, low autonomy ‚Äî then earn autonomy through demonstrated performance.

üñêÔ∏è

Agency

What it CAN do

The range of actions an agent is capable of performing.

A high-agency agent can send emails, update records, create tickets, schedule meetings, analyze data, and generate reports.

Property analogy: A maintenance tech who is certified in plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and appliances has high agency ‚Äî they can fix almost anything.
üîë

Autonomy

What it's ALLOWED to do alone

The degree to which an agent can act without human approval.

A high-autonomy agent decides when, how, and whether to act. A low-autonomy agent recommends and waits for a human to say "go."

Property analogy: That same tech might need manager approval for any repair over $200. They have the skills (agency) but not the authority (autonomy) to act alone on big decisions.

üîÑ Progressive Autonomy (Model C)

Phase 1 üîç

AUDIT ‚Äî Workflow Discovery & Outcome Mapping

Where are we now, and where do we want to be?
‚ñæ
üß† The Analogy

A GPS needs two inputs: your current location and your destination. Give it only one and it can't generate directions. The Audit provides both ‚Äî mapping the current state and defining the target outcomes.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Trap to Avoid

If you only map how humans currently do the work, you'll design agents that mimic human inefficiency ‚Äî the "jet engine on a horse carriage" problem. Map outcomes, not just steps.

Key Activities
  • Workflow Mapping: Document every step, trigger, system, decision point, and pain point in the current process
  • System & Data Landscape: Identify all systems, what data they hold, and whether they can talk to each other
  • Stakeholder Interviews: Talk to new hires, managers, HR team, and (if available) people who left within 90 days
  • Gap Analysis: Score confidence across six dimensions ‚Äî formal steps, workarounds, decisions, handoffs, location variance, outcome validation
  • Outcome Definition: For each step, define the desired outcome (not just the output) ‚Äî "new hire feels committed" vs. "paperwork completed"
üè¢ At RISE

We mapped five onboarding workflows (pre-boarding through feedback aggregation) across three disconnected systems (HRIS, LMS, ticketing) and discovered the core problem: not a systems problem, but a missing orchestration layer.

Key Question for Your Team

"What's the difference between what this step produces (the output) and what we actually want (the outcome)? Where is that gap largest?"

üì¶ Output
Workflow maps with pain points, desired outcomes, system landscape, stakeholder insights, gap analysis, and confidence scores. This becomes the foundation for everything that follows.

GAUGE ‚Äî Readiness Scoring & Prioritization

How ready is each workflow for agent involvement?
‚ñæ
üß† The Analogy

A doctor doesn't treat every symptom at once. They triage ‚Äî assess severity, readiness for treatment, and risk of intervention. Gauge is your triage system for deciding what to automate first, what needs preparation, and what should stay human.

Two Critical Questions

"Can we automate this?" (readiness scoring)
is different from
"Should we automate this?" (ethical assessment)

Gauge answers both.

Eight Readiness Dimensions
  • Impact: How much value does improving this create? (business + human impact)
  • Repeatability: How standardized and rule-based is it?
  • Complexity: How many systems, decisions, and exceptions are involved?
  • Data Readiness: Is the data structured, accessible, and machine-readable?
  • System Readiness: Can the systems actually integrate?
  • Risk Tolerance: What happens if the agent makes an error?
  • Decision Codifiability: Can decisions be expressed as clear rules?
  • Stakeholder Confidence: Do the people involved trust this approach?
üè¢ At RISE

Pre-boarding scored highest on readiness (structured, rule-based, high impact). Role-specific training scored lowest on system readiness (tribal knowledge, no codified standards). This told us where to start.

Ethical Layer (HDSR Framework)

Five ethical questions scored 1‚Äì5 for each workflow: Does it improve experience? Could it cause harm? Does the person retain choice? Will outcomes be equitable? Can decisions be explained? Score below 18/25 = redesign before engineering.

üì¶ Output
Readiness scores for each workflow, ethical assessment, prioritized engineering roadmap ‚Äî a clear answer to "what do we build first and why."

ENGINEER ‚Äî System Design & Human-Agent Role Mapping

Who does what ‚Äî human vs. agent?
‚ñæ
üß† The Analogy

An architect doesn't just draw a building ‚Äî they specify which walls are load-bearing, where the plumbing runs, and which rooms need emergency exits. Engineer specifies exactly how humans and agents work together, where the handoffs happen, and what the safety systems are.

The Design Principle

The agent owns the orchestration. Humans own the relationships, judgment, and exceptions. Nobody's role is diminished ‚Äî the HR coordinator doesn't become less important, they become more important, doing more valuable work with better information.

Five Operational Dimensions (for every agent-involved step)
  • Transparency: How is the agent's work made visible? People can't trust what they can't see.
  • Intervention Paths: When and how can humans step in? Intervention is a feature, not a failure.
  • Governance: What controls ensure safe, fair operation? Rules need version control and approval workflows.
  • Role Redefinition: How do human roles change? The goal is elevation ‚Äî repetitive work moves to agent, judgment work expands for humans.
  • Training & Enablement: What do people need to learn to work alongside agents? Agent literacy is not optional.
üè¢ At RISE

We designed human-agent role maps for pre-boarding: agent handles sequencing, tracking, and reminders. HR handles exceptions, judgment calls, and quality oversight. Managers handle relationships and coaching. Each role is elevated, not replaced.

Progressive Autonomy (Model C)

Agents start at Level 1 (recommend, human approves everything) and earn more autonomy through demonstrated performance. Five levels from full human approval to autonomous execution ‚Äî with the ability to pull back at any point.

üì¶ Output
Human-agent role maps, governance framework, interaction designs, progressive autonomy model, training requirements ‚Äî the complete blueprint for how the system works.

NAVIGATE ‚Äî Stakeholder Communication & Change Management

‚ñæ
üß† The Analogy

The best GPS in the world is useless if the driver doesn't trust it and keeps taking their own route. Navigate is how you build the trust that makes people willing to follow the new system ‚Äî not because they're told to, but because they understand why it's better.

The Core Insight

The same change packaged three different ways is three different messages. Executives need ROI. Managers need "what changes for me." New hires need "you're in good hands." Each audience has different concerns, fears, and motivations.

Audience-Specific Communication
  • Executives: "This decouples onboarding quality from manager bandwidth, enabling 3x growth without proportional hiring costs"
  • Property Managers: "This handles the logistics so you can focus on the people. You keep the judgment calls."
  • HR Team: "Your role shifts from paperwork processing to strategic people operations ‚Äî quality architect, not task executor."
  • New Hires: "We use technology to make sure nothing falls through the cracks, so you always know what to expect and who to ask."
üè¢ At RISE

The biggest communication challenge: helping property managers understand that the agent handles coordination so they can focus on relationships and coaching ‚Äî it's not replacing them, it's freeing them from logistics they never wanted to do.

Transparency as Trust Architecture

Transparency isn't just a compliance checkbox ‚Äî it's how you build the trust that makes the entire system work. When people can see what the agent is doing and why, resistance drops and engagement rises.

TRACK ‚Äî Success Definition & Measurement Framework

‚ñæ
üß† The Analogy

The North Star

The system isn't successful when dashboards are green. It's successful when the employees who come through it are visibly, measurably better prepared and more engaged than those who came before. The employee is the product. Everything else is instrumentation.

Three-Tier Measurement Framework
  • Tier 1 ‚Äî Executive ROI: 90-day turnover rate, time-to-productivity, cost-per-hire, Day 1 readiness rate (Monthly)
  • Tier 2 ‚Äî Operational Efficiency: Pre-boarding completion rate, manager hours reclaimed, check-in completion rate, new hire satisfaction (Weekly)
  • Tier 3 ‚Äî Transformation Sustainability: Pattern detection rate, insights acted upon, location equity index, cohort-over-cohort improvement (Quarterly)

Track isn't the end ‚Äî it's the mechanism that sends the framework back to the beginning. A metric misses target ‚Üí re-Audit. A pattern emerges ‚Üí re-Gauge. A human-agent interaction breaks ‚Üí re-Engineer. Stakeholders resist ‚Üí re-Navigate. The cycle never stops.
